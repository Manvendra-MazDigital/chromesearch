MUCHNICK: Aviary is a company that creates multimedia online editing software. Whether you want do image editing, vector editing, audio editing, or more right in your browser, we provide the software to do it. Originally, Aviary started with a company called worth1000.com which is a competition site where artists can compete in fun image editing contests. And we created software to help serve the needs of that community and realized it could expand from there so that anyone can do image editing online. What is your extension about? MUCHNICK: Aviary's Chrome extension allows anyone to capture a screenshot of any web page your are on, quickly and easily right from within Chrome. You can then mark it up using Aviary's editor — let say add arrows, any kind of text mark up, and you can crop it, rotate it around, do anything you want with it, and then quickly share it with your friends. We're hoping to extend the usefulness of the extension, so that it can capture full page content not just what's on the screen natively. Additionally, we have a few other plans to make it easier to edit images directly off of any web page you're on.

How did you create the screenshot functionality of your extension? MUCHNICK: Our extension takes a screenshot of the page and makes it editable on aviary.com. To capture the screenshot, we use Chrome's capture visible tab method. To make it available to most of the Flash base Aviary tools, we upload the image data to aviary.com via XML HTTP request. For one of the tools, we create a local conduit to make the image data directly available to the Flash application without uploading it to aviary.com at all. What got you interested in Google Chrome extensions? MUCHNICK: What got us interested in Chrome extensions was really how — first of all, how quickly Chrome works and how smoothly all of Aviary's tools operate on it. We've been using Chrome since day one because it really enables our development to go so smoothly.

We work with web pages all the time and we really do a lot of screen capture and mark up internally in our office. So for us to now take that Chrome extension and provide it to our user base was a no-brainer. And additionally, if it encourages Aviary's users to get a better experience because they're using the Chrome browser, well that's a — that's a huge win for us. What was your experience developing Google Chrome extensions? MUCHNICK: Our experience developing Chrome extensions was — was great, it was extremely simple to develop and deploy. And we love the fact that once you developed it, there's no sandbox to hold you back. You simply put it up on Google's website and it is immediately updated and deployed for all your users. When you're stuck in a sandbox it can really cripple your development and rapid innovation. And we're super happy that Google recognizes that.

How did your vision evolve as you were working on your extension? MUCHNICK: The product evolved the entire time we were working on the extension. We had to resign ourselves to the fact that certain elements weren't necessarily complete in the API. But we were able to find workarounds. What were some challenges you faced when writing your extension? MUCHNICK: There were some issues that we had to cope with when developing our extension and I think we came up with some innovative solutions. For example, trying to build out a menu for — when someone clicks on that browser action icon, we had to come up with that ourselves and actually simulate a menu pop-up. Additionally, we had two things; we really had to simulate full page screen capture, so we came up with a way of rolling down the page and taking snapshots as we went using JavaScript and then reassembling it. And lastly, we had to do some filter clean up on some of the JPEG full page screen capture. What do you think about Google Chrome's decision to use CSS, HTML, and JavaScript as the basis of its extension framework? MUCHNICK: We love Chrome's decision to use HTML, JavaScript, and CSS in creating extensions. It makes it so much simpler for everyone on our development team to pick up and run with. Additionally, it seems like this method means the browser is free to operate without additional bloat prior to the extension being invoked.

How many times have you updated your extension? MUCHNICK: We've updated our extension twice so far and the experience was fantastic. Once we figured out how to get the manifest correctly uploaded it was a snap and it was instantly deployed and available for our users. How did you promote your extension? MUCHNICK: To promote our extension we updated our user base on our own blog and newsletter and shared it with relevant tech blogs. We also plan to promote it in context within our existing product set. What advice can you give someone before starting to work on a Google Chrome extension? MUCHNICK: If you're working on a Chrome extension, consider the overall architecture of your extension before you begin. Especially how you will divide up responsibilities and communicate between the background page, content scripts, and more.
